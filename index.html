The Weasel and the Stoat

By Mustelid Enthusiast ‚Ä¢ October 24, 2023

It is a common confusion in the British countryside: a flash of russet fur, a long body, and a scamper into the undergrowth. Was that a weasel or a stoat? While they are close cousins in the mustelid family, there are distinct differences that can help the keen observer tell them apart.

The Golden Rule

"A weasel is weasily identified, and a stoat is stoatally different."

1. The Tail

The most reliable indicator is the tail. A stoat's tail is longer and always ends in a distinctive bushy black tip. This black tip remains even in winter when the stoat turns white (ermine). The weasel's tail is much shorter and is entirely the same colour as its back (russet brown).

2. Size and Stature

Stoats are generally larger than weasels. A stoat is roughly the size of a thin rat, whereas a weasel is smaller, closer to the size of a large mouse or a cigar. However, a large male weasel can sometimes be confused with a small female stoat if distance makes size judging difficult.

3. Movement

While both animals are energetic, the stoat has a distinctive bounding gait, often arching its back as it moves. Weasels tend to run flat against the ground, almost flowing over the terrain like a liquid.

References: The Mammal Society, UK Wildlife Trust.
